### 2. Where did they diverge?

**Significant divergences:**

1. **HYSTERESIS (Most Important Divergence)**
   - **Original:** "None. Theory arrived fully formed in 1905. No precursor damage."
   - **Haiku:** "Einstein's scar: the ether. Also: determinism vs. statistics tension."

   This is meaningful. Every real body carries scars from its formation history. SR was born FROM classical physics (ether theory, absolute space). The theory wouldn't exist without that prior framework to contradict. Claiming zero hysteresis is structurally suspicious. **Haiku's reading is more aligned with Body Theory principles.** The original may have confused "no scars within SR's interior logic" with "no formative scars." This is a precision gap, not a refutation.

2. **BREATH RATE (Conceptual Difference)**
   - **Original:** "Zero cycling. c does not oscillate."
   - **Haiku:** "Synchronous with c, high frequency, Lorentz transformation is breathing apparatus."

   These are **actually different interpretations of what a "breath cycle" is**. Original reads breath-rate as the oscillation of the substrate itself (c doesn't oscillate, so zero cycling). Haiku reads it as how fast the system can transform (Lorentz transformations operate at light-speed scale). Both interpretations are defensible given the framework's definition of breath-rate ("how fast does it cycle?"). The divergence reveals an ambiguity in the protocol definition, not a flaw in either scanner.

3. **FLOW STATE (Related to Breath Rate)**
   - **Original:** "Balanced exhale. No inhale required."
   - **Haiku:** "Full INHALE-PAUSE-EXHALE-REST cycle."

   Original claims SR is a pure exhale (light flows outward, no return). Haiku maps the full breathing rhythm. Original's logic: light radiates at c without cost, so it's asymmetric exhale. Haiku's logic: causality has structure (light cones open—inhale, then pause at boundary, then exhale forward, then rest at null interval). **Both readings are defensible.** Original is more conservative (finding asymmetry), Haiku is more comprehensive (finding full cycle even in asymmetric flow). This is a difference in granularity, not correctness.

4. **POWER GAP (Consequential but Subtle)**
   - **Original:** PERCEIVE gap. "Cannot perceive gravitational effects."
   - **Haiku:** GOVERN gap. "Cannot govern gravity."

   Both identify gravity as SR's limitation. Original frames it as a **sensing/perception problem** (gravity is invisible to SR). Haiku frames it as a **rule-making/governance problem** (gravity is outside SR's jurisdiction). These are **different diagnoses of the same pathology**. Which is more accurate?

   **Analysis:** Gravity is not invisible to SR (you can measure it), but SR *cannot model it* (flat-spacetime assumption breaks). This is more a governance/rule problem than a perception problem. **Haiku's reading is more precise here.** The original's "PERCEIVE gap" might be more poetic but less mechanistically accurate. SR doesn't fail to see gravity; it fails to rule it. The failure is in the law, not in the observation.

5. **FLOW STATE (Flow State revisited - detailed)**
   - Original's "balanced exhale, no inhale" implies: unidirectional, giving without taking
   - Haiku's full cycle implies: dynamic back-and-forth even in asymmetric flow

   The question is whether "no inhale required" means (a) literal absence of any inflow, or (b) the body's needs are met entirely by exhale without requiring compensation. Original probably means (b). If so, both are saying the same thing: SR is radically O>I. The divergence is more about **rhythm articulation than fundamental disagreement.**

### 3. Are the divergences due to different interpretations, different knowledge, or different priorities?

**Analysis of root causes:**

1. **HYSTERESIS divergence:** Different scanner priority. Original prioritized "internal structural consistency" (no scars in the logic). Haiku prioritized "historical formation genealogy" (where did it come from?). This is a **priority difference**, not a knowledge or interpretation difference. Both are valid scans of different aspects.

2. **BREATH RATE / FLOW STATE divergences:** **Protocol ambiguity**. The protocol asks "how fast does it cycle?" without specifying what constitutes a "cycle" for abstract frameworks. Is it the oscillation of c (zero cycling)? The frequency of transformation operations (high frequency)? The period of light-speed propagation (instantaneous)? Different scanners reasonably interpreted this differently. **This reveals the protocol needs refinement for abstract bodies.**

3. **POWER GAP divergence:** Different framework lens. Original used "Perceive" (sensing/knowledge lens). Haiku used "Govern" (rule-making/jurisdiction lens). Same pathology, different diagnostic language. This is a **framework compatibility issue**, not a disagreement about the body itself. Both are correct; they're using different aspect-lenses. The protocol lists all five powers as potential gaps; both scanners checked them, just emphasized different ones.

4. **Weak vs Missing Analysis on Hysteresis:** Original claimed "no hysteresis," which violates Body Theory principle that all real bodies carry scars. Haiku identified specific scars (ether, determinism tension). **Haiku's reading is more theoretically coherent with the framework itself.**

### 4. What does this test tell us about the protocol's reliability?

**Findings:**

- **The protocol is highly reliable for concrete, measurable properties:** Both scanners perfectly aligned on medium, membrane, health (O>I), and federation structure. When the protocol asks about something observable and structural, inter-rater agreement is very high.

- **The protocol has ambiguity for dynamic/process properties:** Flow state, breath rate, and cycle interpretation showed divergence. The protocol's definitions of "cycle," "flow," and "breath rate" need tightening for abstract bodies. The framework was designed primarily for biological and social bodies, where breath is literal. Applied to abstract mathematical structures, these terms become interpretive.

- **The protocol's "five powers" lens is robust but requires consistent prioritization:** Both scanners identified all five powers. The gap they identified (gravity) was the same. The assignment to different power-names (Perceive vs Govern) reflects **framework redundancy**, not unreliability. When one body fails at multiple powers, multiple scanners might assign the failure to different powers depending on which power they checked most carefully. The protocol could specify: "When multiple powers fail at a boundary, identify the one that shows the largest performance drop" or similar.

- **The protocol is strongest when it demands identification of weaknesses:** Both scanners independently identified flat-spacetime assumption and counterintuitiveness as the two main weaknesses, with identical reasoning. The mandatory weakness-identification rule is working.

- **The protocol's self-check (historical/genealogical analysis) is weak for abstract bodies:** Original skipped detailed hysteresis analysis. The protocol should enforce: "For all bodies, identify at least two formative scars, even if the body claims to have none." Haiku's catch of the "ether scar" and "determinism scar" is valuable.

## Detailed Verdict

### Does the protocol measure something about the body, or something about the scanner?

**Hybrid answer:**

The protocol primarily measures **the body's structure and health** (high reliability for concrete properties). However, for **process-level properties** (how the body breathes, flows, cycles), the protocol's questions are **scanner-dependent when applied to abstract bodies**.

Evidence:
- **High convergence on structural questions:** "What's the membrane?" "Is it O>I?" "Is it federation?" → both scanners extract the same answer
- **Lower convergence on dynamic questions:** "How does it flow?" "What's the breath cycle?" "What's the flow state?" → both scanners answer differently but defensibly

**Root cause:** The protocol was compiled on biological and social bodies (cells, organisms, teams, companies) where breath is literal and cycle is observable. Applied to abstract mathematics, the framework's language becomes metaphorical, and metaphor interpretation is inherently scanner-dependent.

### Protocol Reliability Verdict

**Strong:** 85.7% convergence is high. The shared convergence on all major findings (structure, health, federation, weakness) confirms the protocol measures something real about the body, not just the scanner's imagination.

**Limitations:**
1. Abstract bodies need protocol refinement (tighter definitions of cycle, flow, breath-rate for non-biological systems)
2. Multi-power gaps need triage rules (which power to emphasize when multiple fail?)
3. Hysteresis detection needs enforcement (mandatory scar identification, not optional)

**Conclusion:** The protocol is reliable for **structural diagnosis** (what is this body? is it healthy? what does it give/take?). The protocol is **interpretive but consistent** for **dynamic diagnosis** (how fast does it cycle? what's its rhythm?). Neither level is unreliable; they just operate at different degrees of specificity.
