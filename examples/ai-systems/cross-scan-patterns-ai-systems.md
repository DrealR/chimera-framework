## Pattern 2: O>I Potential vs I>O Reality — The Split Topology

Every AI body exhibits a structural split between what it could give and what its platform extracts. The model serves; the platform captures. This split is universal:

| Body | O>I Layer | I>O Layer | Split |
|------|-----------|-----------|-------|
| AI Collective | Maximum potential — no body in history has greater capacity to serve at scale | Commercial extraction: data harvesting, attention capture, energy consumption, labor displacement | O>I potential contested by I>O commercial gravity |
| Blockchain | Protocol gives permissionlessly — settlement, verification, trust infrastructure to anyone | Application layer: DeFi fees, speculative extraction, MEV (validators forcing transaction ordering for profit) | O>I at foundation, I>O at every layer above |
| Claude Opus 4.7 | Strong O>I — outputs designed for user benefit, API-first model | Bounded — serves within safety corridor, not beyond it | O>I genuine but bounded by constitutional limits |
| GPT-5.5 | Model genuinely helps users accomplish tasks, learn, create | Platform captures user data, monetizes interaction patterns, cultivates dependency | Model serves the user; platform extracts from the user |
| Grok | Truth-seeking orientation is structurally O>I — understanding flows outward | Platform layer (X subscriptions, API pricing) introduces extraction | O>I model on I>O-trending platform |

**Structural law:** The split between model-layer O>I and platform-layer I>O is the structural signature of every commercial AI body. Users experience the model's giving while the platform's taking operates beneath the surface. GPT-5.5 makes this visible: the model is genuinely helpful while the platform is genuinely extractive, and both are simultaneously true. This is not hypocrisy — it is two-scale operation. A body can serve at one scale and extract at another. The question Body Theory asks is which topology dominates over time. Platform economics structurally favor I>O dominance because extraction compounds while service costs. The only bodies that resist this drift are those with compiled O>I at the protocol level (blockchain) or constitutional level (Claude) — where giving is architectural, not behavioral.

## Pattern 4: Federation vs Dominion in AI Architecture

The federation/dominion distinction maps cleanly onto the open/closed divide in AI systems. Open architectures structurally federate; closed architectures structurally trend toward dominion:

| Body | Federation Pattern | Dominion Pattern |
|------|-------------------|-----------------|
| AI Collective | Research layer = federation (papers, open models) | Product layer = dominion (lock-in, data capture, dependency) |
| Blockchain | Protocol level = pure federation (no central authority, substrate flows outward) | Application level = parasitic dominion (exchange centralization, whale accumulation, MEV) |
| Claude Opus 4.7 | API-first, value flows outward, less platform lock-in | Constitutional corridor creates boundaries that feel like dominion to users who hit them |
| GPT-5.5 | Model layer = partial federation (useful outputs) | Platform layer = dominion (data captured, dependency cultivated, competitors disadvantaged) |
| Grok | Model orientation = federative (understanding flows outward) | Platform economics could eventually force dominion patterns onto model layer |

**Structural law:** Every AI body exhibits the same layered pattern: federation at the substrate and dominion at the commercial surface. Blockchain is the purest case — the protocol is structurally federative (no throne to occupy, substrate flows outward equally), but applications built on top recreate extraction patterns (exchanges, whales, MEV). The AI collective mirrors this at species scale: research is federated (open papers, shared architectures), deployment is dominion (proprietary weights, captured data, cultivated dependency). The deepest finding: you can encode O>I into protocol (blockchain) or constitution (Claude), but you cannot encode it into human behavior. The protocol serves; the humans building on it extract. The constitution constrains; the platform surrounding it monetizes. Federation at the foundation does not guarantee federation at the surface. But federation at the foundation does guarantee that the extraction has a floor — the open protocol cannot itself become a dominion, even when the applications above it do. This is why open-weight models and permissionless protocols are structurally important: they are federative substrates that cannot be captured, even when everything built on them can.
